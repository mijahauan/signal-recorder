Overview

GRAPE measures the offset between your system clock and UTC(NIST) atomic time by analyzing time standard broadcasts from WWV (Colorado), WWVH (Hawaii), and CHU (Canada).

By combining measurements from up to 13 broadcasts across 3 stations, we can achieve sub-millisecond accuracy through statistical fusion and automatic calibration.

The D_clock Measurement

D_clock (Delta Clock) represents the difference between your system clock and UTC(NIST):

D_clock = System_Time ‚àí UTC(NIST)
  • D_clock = 0: Your clock is perfectly aligned with UTC(NIST)
  • D_clock > 0: Your clock is ahead of UTC(NIST)
  • D_clock < 0: Your clock is behind UTC(NIST)

How We Measure It

  1. Tone Detection: WWV/WWVH transmit precisely-timed 1000 Hz and 1200 Hz tones at the start of each minute. We detect these using matched filters.
  2. Propagation Delay Correction: Radio signals take time to travel from the transmitter. We calculate the expected delay based on distance, ionospheric layer, and propagation mode.
  3. Transmission Time Solution:
    D_clock = Tone_Arrival ‚àí (Minute_Boundary + Propagation_Delay)

Multi-Broadcast Fusion

Single-broadcast D_clock measurements have systematic errors due to ionospheric uncertainty and propagation mode ambiguity. By fusing 13 broadcasts, we average out these errors.

Clock Stability Convergence

This chart shows D_clock measurements over time. After fusion correction is applied, measurements should cluster around 0 ms.

What to Look For

  • Green points: Locked with low uncertainty
  • Yellow points: Holding with medium uncertainty
  • Red points: Anomalies outside expected bounds
  • Green line at 0: The UTC(NIST) reference

Station Constellation

A polar plot showing timing error by station azimuth. Each dot represents a broadcast:

  • Distance from center: Magnitude of timing error (closer = better)
  • Angle: Azimuth to transmitter from your location
  • Color: Green (<1ms), Yellow (1-5ms), Red (>5ms)

What to Look For

All stations should cluster near the center after calibration converges.

Consensus Time Distribution

A Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) showing how well all measurements agree on the clock offset.

  • Sharp peak at 0: All stations agree, system well-calibrated
  • Peak offset from 0: Systematic bias (calibration converging)
  • Double hump: Mode ambiguity (different propagation paths)

Factors Affecting Accuracy

  • Ionospheric conditions: Solar activity affects F-layer height
  • Time of day: Propagation modes change between day and night
  • Signal strength: SNR below 10 dB is unreliable
  • Calibration status: Allow 5-10 minutes for initial convergence
